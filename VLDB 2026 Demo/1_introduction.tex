\section{Introduction} 
Visualizing data transformations on dashboards are widely used by companies and
non-profit organizations in various industries to inform decision making. By
summarizing large volumes of data into visual trends and aggregated metrics,
dashboards enable users to monitor system performance and detect patterns or
anomalies. However, these visualizations typically present only the final
aggregated value of metrics. The intermediate computation steps, such as the
underlying data sources, transformation logic, and component metrics, are
hidden. When the context or derivation of a metric is unclear, users may
misinterpret trends, struggle to diagnose anomalies, or lose trust in the
reported numbers~\cite{}. Therefore, improving transparency into how metrics are
computed, including their intermediate components and data provenance, is
critical for helping users accurately interpret dashboard visualizations and
make informed decisions.

To improve transparency in data pipelines, data lineage and provenance systems
have been developed to support data quality monitoring and governance of modern
data pipelines. Platforms such as Collibra~\cite{} and OpenMetadata~\cite{}
capture dependencies between datasets, tables, and attributes, enabling data
professionals to trace the origin and flow of data. While lineage graphs can
reveal where a metric's underlying data originates, the transformation logic
that defines the metric, such as aggregations, filters, joins, and derived
submetrics, often remains in SQL models or ETL scripts. As a result,
understanding how a metric is computed still requires inspecting source code,
which creates additional effort even for technical users.

Moreover, existing lineage systems focus on dataset-level lineage rather than
explaining the semantics of transformations that define business metrics. This
limitation is more pronounced for non-technical stakeholders, who care less
about which tables or columns produce a metric but more about how to interpret
the metric itself. In practice, many analytic questions begin with a dashboard
metric (e.g., ``Why did revenue drop?'') rather than a dataset. To support the
trust of data-driven decision-making, provenance information must move beyond
data-centric lineage and provide accessible, metric-centric explanations that
help non-technical users understand how complex metrics are derived. We
highlight the need for a system that shows lineage and provenance of metrics.

Above example shows that even if the dashboard can show the metric lineage and
the transformation steps, it still requires users to understand what those
metrics mean, and how they are computed. To understand the meaning of a metric,
users need to understand the transformation steps that define the metric, and
how those steps relate to the final metric. This process is tedious and requires
users to understand the technical details of the transformation logic, which can
be a barrier for non-technical users. Therefore, there is a need for a system
that can automatically extract and visualize the transformation steps that
define a metric, and provide explanations of what those steps mean in terms of
the final metric. Such a system would help users understand how metrics are
computed, and improve their trust in the reported numbers.

We introduce \clue, a system that automatically extracts and visualizes the
transformation steps that define a metric, and provides explanations of what
those steps mean in terms of the final metric. \clue captures the transformation
logic that defines a metric, and visualizes the metric lineage and the
transformation steps in an interactive dashboard. Users can explore the metric
lineage and the transformation steps, and see how each step contributes to the
final metric.

Provenance/lineage graphs~\cite{DBLP:journals/tods/ChapmanLMT24,
DBLP:journals/tvcg/CaiGXYWXWLW25,DBLP:conf/deem/PinaCK0M24} show the data flow
and transformations in various domains such as
databases~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-0912-5340, DBLP:conf/icdt/BunemanKT01},
and machine learning pipelines~\cite{DBLP:conf/deem/PinaCK0M24,
DBLP:journals/tods/ChapmanLMT24}. However, while they often focus on changes in
the data and transformations, they do not discuss what these transformations
mean in terms of metrics, and how the transformation meanings are related to the
final results. 
% visualizing data transformation
Visualization of intermediate
transformations~\cite{DBLP:journals/pvldb/LeeTABCKMSYHP21,
DBLP:conf/chi/WangLDM025} allow users to transform and explore data
interactively. DataFormulator2~\cite{DBLP:conf/chi/WangLDM025} provides an
environment for transforming and visualizing intermediate results, while
Lux~\cite{DBLP:journals/pvldb/LeeTABCKMSYHP21} surfaces intermediate insights
directly in Jupyter notebooks. However, neither discusses how transformations
relate to the final results in terms of metrics.
